Lazy Light Switch

Lights off!


I built this silly little thing to combat the problem of somone coming into your room while you are all comfortable in bed, turning on your light, and leaving without turning it off. Using a Basic Stamp board, some wires, a servo, a piece of lego, and a little code, I made an extension of my light switch that sits beside my bed. This project was to both serve a purpose for me and to learn a little about building circuits. I learned how to wire up the buttons and resistors used for the switch, and wrote the code to detect when I pressed either the up or down button and what position to move the servo to. I chopped the ends off an old ethernet cord to run the wires across my ceiling to the servo, and instead of wasting 9 volt batteries by always having the switch on, I took a 9 volt wall adapter and soldered a 9 volt battery connection to it which allowed me to power it via a wall outlet.

Performance Exhaust

Designing a race car exhaust.


In my first year at the Univerisity of Guelph, I joined the Formula SAE Racing team. A team member and I were tasked with building a new exhaust system for the teams 2017 car. The previous exhaust system did not meet the sound level and performance requirements, so the challenge was to design a system that performed within those constraints. We calculated the required lengths, and diameters of the tubing need for our exhaust system to meet the need performance and sound specifications. My team member and I created the final design in SolidWorks, and once approved by a team lead, built it in the team's shop and mounted it to the car. The exhaust ended up meeting all the required specifications it was designed for, including reducing sound while increasing horsepower and torque.

Clutch Replacement

Replacing that 25 year old clutch.


Replacing the clutch requires separating the transmission from the engine. On a Miata, this requires removing the exhaust, driveshaft, and power plant frame. I upgraded the clutch to a Flyin' Miata Stage 1 clutch, which should be more than suitable for any power upgrades I make to the car in the future.

Upgrading Suspension

Replacing cheap coilovers with quality coilovers.


This was my second time replacing the suspension since moving away from the stock suspension. I initially replaced the suspension with cheap coilovers (red shock) when I first got the car. Coilovers allow adjustable ride height with relative ease, and after I purchased the car I immediately wanted that low race car feel that coilovers offer. With my bank account running low from just purchasing the car, I opted for the cheap route. I soon came to regret that. While I got the low race car feel I wanted, I sacrificed ride quality by going the cheap route. I decided to fix my mistake by upgrading to some very quality Tein Flex-Z Coilovers (green shock). Offering all the bells and whistles one could ask for in coilovers, from adjustable dampening, ride height, and pre-load.

Radiator Replacement

Just chillin'


The stock radiator (right) has had its day. Indicated by the color of the plastic on top of the radiator, the browner the plastic, the more likely it is to fail. I took this opportunity to upgrade it to a Koyo radiator (left). This radiator is a bit overkill for the current motor but should be able to handle any performance modifications I throw at the motor in the future. While replacing the radiator, I did a coolant flush shown in the bottom picture (first flush on the left, moving right). This involves running distilled water through the engine multiple times until it drains clear. This will clean and remove any build up in the motor, then proceed by replacing it with fresh coolant.

Light Restoration

Ka-chow


The plastic from the taillights was old and faded, given they are 25 years old. I removed the tail lights from the car, and sanded down the plastic with sandpaper, in steps starting from 600 grit up to 2000 grit (middle photo). After sanding, I broke out the buffer and polished them with a rubbing compound, leaving a beautiful, brand new looking shine that should last another 25 years. The smallest details make the biggest difference.
